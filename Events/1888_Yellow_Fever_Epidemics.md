# Yellow Fever in Florida: The Scourge of the Tropics
## 1888 and Earlier

---

## Overview

For centuries, yellow fever—called "Yellow Jack," "Bronze John," and "the saffron scourge"—haunted Florida like no other disease, striking down thousands and paralyzing communities every summer and fall. The 1888 epidemic in Jacksonville, which killed over 400 people and drove 10,000 residents to flee the city, was the last major outbreak in Florida history. Before the discovery that mosquitoes transmitted the disease, yellow fever seemed to strike randomly, killing some while sparing others, emptying cities, destroying economies, and creating such terror that healthy communities would shoot at refugees trying to escape infected areas. The conquest of yellow fever through mosquito control was one of the great public health triumphs of the early 20th century.

---

## The Disease

### What Is Yellow Fever

**The Virus:**
- Viral hemorrhagic disease
- Transmitted by Aedes aegypti mosquito
- Not understood until 1900
- Deadly and terrifying

**The Symptoms:**
- Sudden onset
- High fever, headache
- Jaundice (yellowing skin—hence the name)
- "Black vomit" (internal bleeding)
- Death in severe cases

### The Terror

**The Fear:**
- Seemed to strike randomly
- No cure
- High mortality (20-50% in epidemics)
- Caused panic

**The Names:**
- "Yellow Jack" (from quarantine flag)
- "Bronze John"
- "The Saffron Scourge"
- "Black Vomit"

### The Outbreak

**The Beginning:**
- August 1888
- First cases appeared
- Spread rapidly
- Panic ensued

**The Source:**
- Tampa travelers believed cause
- Ship traffic
- Mosquito transmission (unknown then)
- Fast spread

### The Flight

**The Exodus:**
- 10,000 fled Jacksonville

- City paralyzed

**The Refugees:**
- Other cities refused them
- Shotgun quarantines

- Desperate wanderers

**The Response:**

**The Enforcement:**
- Neighboring towns armed
- Refugees shot at

- Suffering intense

## The Understanding

### Before 1900

**The Theories:**
- "Miasma" (bad air)
- Filth and garbage
- Contagion person-to-person
- All wrong

**The Methods:**
- Fumigation
- Burning clothes
- Isolating patients
- Partially effective (accidentally killed some mosquitoes)

### The Discovery

**Walter Reed (1900):**
- Proved mosquito transmission
- Yellow Fever Commission in Cuba
- Aedes aegypti identified
- Revolutionary understanding

**Carlos Finlay:**
- Cuban physician
- First proposed mosquito theory (1881)
- Reed confirmed
- True discoverer

### The Conquest

**The Methods:**
- Mosquito control
- Drainage programs
- Screening
- Eliminate breeding

**The Success:**
- Last major U.S. outbreak 1905 (New Orleans)
- Florida freed from terror
- Public health triumph
- Disease controlled
