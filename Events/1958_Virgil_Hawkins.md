# Virgil Hawkins and the Desegregation of Florida's Universities
## 1949-1976

---

## Overview

In 1949, Virgil Darnell Hawkins, a 42-year-old Black man from Okahumpka, Florida, applied to the University of Florida Law School and was denied admission because of his race. He would spend the next 27 years fighting for justiceâ€”not for himself, since he was eventually too old to attend, but for those who came after him. Hawkins's case went to the U.S. Supreme Court four times, and his persistence helped break down segregation in Florida's public universities. Though the state fought him for decades, other Black students were finally admitted while his own case dragged on. Hawkins eventually earned his law degree elsewhere and practiced briefly before his death, but his real legacy was opening the doors he was never allowed to walk through himself.

## The Application

### 1949

**The Attempt:**
- Applied to University of Florida College of Law
- Qualified academically
- Denied because of race
- Separate but "equal" was law

**The Response:**
- Florida offered to pay out-of-state tuition
- Black students could attend elsewhere
- Hawkins refused
- Wanted Florida to change

### The Legal Battle Begins

**The Lawsuit:**
- Filed suit in Florida Supreme Court
- NAACP provided support
- Thurgood Marshall involved
- Case: *Hawkins v. Board of Control*

**The Opposition:**
- State of Florida fought vigorously
- Delay after delay
- Every legal maneuver used
- Determined to maintain segregation

## The Supreme Court Cases

### First Appeal (1954)

**The Context:**
- Brown v. Board of Education decided May 17, 1954
- Segregation unconstitutional
- Hawkins hoped for victory
- Sent back to Florida courts

**The Ruling:**
- U.S. Supreme Court remanded case
- Florida must reconsider in light of Brown
- Seemed like victory
- Florida resisted

### Second Appeal (1956)

**Florida's Response:**
- Florida Supreme Court delayed
- Cited need for "gradual adjustment"
- Denied Hawkins admission again
- Said tensions too high

**U.S. Supreme Court Response:**
- Hawkins appealed again
- Court ruled: Brown doesn't require gradual adjustment for graduate schools
- Hawkins entitled to "prompt" admission
- Sent back to Florida again

### Third Appeal (1957)

**More Delay:**
- Florida still refused
- Created new barriers
- Required new tests
- Endless obstacles

**The Pattern:**
- Each victory in Washington
- Followed by resistance in Tallahassee
- Years passing
- Hawkins aging

### Fourth Appeal (1958)

**The Exhaustion:**
- Hawkins appealed yet again
- Supreme Court again ruled for him
- But by now nearly a decade had passed
- Victory hollow

## The Resolution

### Hawkins Withdraws

**1958:**
- After nine years of fighting
- Hawkins withdrew his application
- Too old, too exhausted
- Focus on broader change

**The Statement:**
- Didn't want admission if others wouldn't benefit
- Victory must be permanent
- Not just personal
- Sacrificed for principle

### Other Students Admitted

**George Starke Jr. (1958):**
- First Black student at UF
- Admitted to law school
- Hawkins's struggle opened door
- Bitter irony

**Integration Proceeds:**
- Other Black students followed
- Slow but irreversible
- Hawkins's legacy realized
