# Geronimo at Fort Pickens: The Apache Prisoners of Pensacola
## 1886-1888

---

## Overview

In October 1886, the legendary Apache warrior Geronimo and his band of fighters arrived in chains at Fort Pickens on Santa Rosa Island near Pensacola, Floridaâ€”as far from their Arizona homeland as the U.S. government could send them while remaining in the country. For eighteen months, Geronimo and his warriors were held as prisoners of war in the old masonry fort, becoming unlikely tourist attractions as thousands of curious Floridians crossed the bay to catch a glimpse of the famous "savage." Their imprisonment at Fort Pickens was part of a shameful chapter in which the government separated Apache families, sending women and children to a different fort in Florida while keeping the warriors isolated. Geronimo would never return to Arizona, dying in captivity in Oklahoma in 1909.

## The Background

### The Apache Wars

**The Conflict:**
- Decades of warfare in Southwest
- Apache resistance to American expansion
- Brutal fighting on both sides
- Geronimo became symbol

**Geronimo:**
- Born 1829, Arizona
- Chiricahua Apache leader
- Family killed by Mexicans
- Legendary warrior and raider

### The Surrender

**September 1886:**
- Geronimo surrendered to General Nelson Miles
- Final Apache resistance ended
- Terms promised imprisonment in Florida
- Then reunion with families

**The Betrayal:**
- Government didn't honor terms
- Families sent to Fort Marion (St. Augustine)
- Warriors sent separately to Fort Pickens
- Deliberate separation

## Fort Pickens

### The Fort

**The Location:**
- Santa Rosa Island
- Entrance to Pensacola Bay
- Isolated position
- Maximum security

**The Structure:**
- Masonry fortification
- Built 1829-1834
- Designed for coastal defense
- Now a prison

### Why Fort Pickens

**The Reasoning:**
- Isolated from other Apaches
- Far from homeland
- Difficult escape
- Symbolic exile

**The Decision:**
- General Phil Sheridan's recommendation
- Punish the warriors
- Separate from "innocent" women and children
- Political considerations
