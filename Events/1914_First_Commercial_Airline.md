# The World's First Commercial Airline Flight
## January 1, 1914: St. Petersburg to Tampa

---

## Overview

On New Year's Day 1914, a small wooden flying boat lifted off from the waters of Tampa Bay, carrying one passenger from St. Petersburg to Tampa. That 23-minute flight across the bay was the world's first scheduled commercial airline flight, and it launched the age of airline travel. The pilot was Tony Jannus, an experienced aviator; the passenger was Abram Pheil, the former mayor of St. Petersburg who had won the seat at auction. The St. Petersburg-Tampa Airboat Line operated for only four months, but it proved that passenger aviation was possible, practical, and profitableâ€”and it all started in Florida.

## The Airline

### The St. Petersburg-Tampa Airboat Line

**The Organization:**
- World's first scheduled airline
- Incorporated in Florida
- City of St. Petersburg backed it
- Private operation with public support

**The Funding:**
- City contributed subsidy
- Private investors
- Ticket sales
- Experimental venture

### The Aircraft

**The Benoist Type XIV:**
- Flying boat (seaplane)
- Designed by Thomas Benoist
- Single engine
- One passenger plus pilot

**The Specifications:**
- 75 horsepower engine
- Wooden construction
- Open cockpit
- Cruised about 60 mph

### The Route

**St. Petersburg to Tampa:**
- Across Tampa Bay
- 18 miles
- 23 minutes flight time
- Water landing at both ends

## The Airline's Operation

### The Schedule

**Daily Service:**
- Two flights per day initially
- St. Petersburg to Tampa
- Tampa to St. Petersburg
- Regular schedule

**The Price:**
- $5 per trip (normal fare)
- Special charter rates
- Comparable to boat fare
- Premium for speed

### The Experience

**The Flight:**
- Open cockpit
- Wind in face
- Water below
- Thrilling

**The Service:**
- 23 minutes vs. hours by land
- Reliability issues (weather)
- Adventurous passengers
- Early adopters

### The End

**Four Months:**
- Operated January-April 1914
- About 1,200 passengers total
- Financially marginal
- Seasonal nature

**The Closure:**
- Tourist season ended
- Subsidy ran out
- Benoist needed plane elsewhere
- Experiment concluded
