# Zora Neale Hurston: The Eatonville Genius
## 1891-1960

---

## Overview

Zora Neale Hurston was one of the most important American writers of the 20th centuryâ€”an anthropologist, novelist, folklorist, and central figure of the Harlem Renaissance who grew up in Eatonville, Florida, the first incorporated Black town in America. Hurston's masterpiece, *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937), was set in Florida and drew on the Black Southern culture she grew up immersed in. Despite her literary achievements, Hurston died in poverty and obscurity in Fort Pierce, Florida, her grave unmarked until Alice Walker found and honored it in 1973. Today she is recognized as a towering figure in American letters, and her Eatonville home is a pilgrimage site for readers who love her work.

## The Life

### Early Years (1891-1917)

**The Family:**
- Born January 7, 1891
- Father: John Hurston (minister, mayor)
- Mother: Lucy Ann Potts Hurston
- Fifth of eight children

**The Loss:**
- Mother died 1904
- Father remarried quickly
- Hurston left home
- Difficult years

### Education and Emergence

**Howard University:**
- Attended 1919-1924
- Began writing
- Developed intellectually
- Met mentors

**Barnard College:**
- Only Black student at time
- Studied anthropology
- Franz Boas as mentor
- Graduated 1928

### The Harlem Renaissance

**The Scene:**
- Arrived New York 1920s
- Central Renaissance figure
- Langston Hughes friendship
- Patronage and connections

**The Work:**

## The Anthropology

### The Collecting

**Florida Fieldwork:**
- Returned South to collect folklore
- Traveled through Black South
- Recorded stories, songs, customs
- Pioneering work

**The Method:**
- Participant observer
- Used her insider status
- Authentic documentation
- Scientific and artistic

### The Results

**Mules and Men (1935):**
- Florida and Louisiana folklore
- Hoodoo documentation
- Stories and customs
- Ground breaking collection

**Tell My Horse (1938):**
- Caribbean research
- Haiti and Jamaica
- Voodoo studies
- Cultural documentation

## The Fiction

### The Masterpiece

**Their Eyes Were Watching God (1937):**
- Written in Haiti in seven weeks
- Janie Crawford's story
- Search for love and self
- Florida settings

**The Significance:**
- Celebrated Black female voice
- Dialect as literary language
- Universal and specific
- Now considered classic

### Other Novels

**Jonah's Gourd Vine (1934):**
- Based on her parents
- Eatonville setting
- Preacher's life
- Personal material

**Moses, Man of the Mountain (1939):**
- Biblical retelling
- Hoodoo elements
- Allegory of slavery
- Ambitious project

**Seraph on the Suwanee (1948):**
- White characters
- Florida setting
- Controversial choice
- Mixed reception

## The Controversy

### The Criticism

**During Her Time:**
- Some Black intellectuals criticized
- Richard Wright attacked her work
- "Minstrel" accusations
- Dialect controversial

**The Arguments:**
- She portrayed joy, not protest
- Dialect seen as demeaning
- Apolitical accusations
- Complex debate

### The Defense

**Her Position:**
- Celebrated Black culture
- Refused victimhood narrative
- Documented folk genius
- Authentic voice

**Later Reassessment:**
- Walker and others championed
- Seen as ahead of time
- Cultural pride recognized
- Canonical status

## The Decline

### The Hard Years

**Post-1948:**
- Publishing difficulties
- Money problems
- False morals charge (1948) - devastating
- Withdrew from public

**The Circumstances:**
- Worked as maid at times
- Stroke in 1959
- County welfare home
- Died January 28, 1960

### The Death

**Fort Pierce, Florida:**
- Died in poverty
- Unmarked grave
- Garden of Heavenly Rest cemetery
- Forgotten
