# The Key West Wrecking Industry
## 1825-1900: Riches from the Reef

---

## Overview

For most of the nineteenth century, Key West was the wealthiest city per capita in the United States, and its fortune came from disaster. Ships sailing between the Atlantic and the Gulf of Mexico had to navigate the treacherous Florida Reef, and many didn't make it. Key West's "wreckers" stood ready to salvage the cargo, rescue the passengers, and claim their legally entitled share of the goods. At its peak, the wrecking industry made Key West Florida's largest city and created an aristocracy of sea captains who built the elegant Victorian homes that still grace the island. It was a peculiar economy built on the misfortune of othersâ€”legal, regulated, and enormously profitable.

## The Wrecking Business

### How It Worked

**The System:**
- Ships wrecked on reef
- Wreckers sailed out first
- First ship on scene got "wreck master" rights
- Organized salvage operation

**The Law:**
- Federal court in Key West
- Judge determined salvage awards
- Usually 25-50% of cargo value
- Legal and regulated

### The Players

**The Wreckers:**
- Licensed by federal court
- Captains with crews
- Fast, shallow-draft vessels
- Competitive business

**The Crews:**
- Multi-ethnic (white, Black, Bahamian)
- Skilled sailors
- Hard, dangerous work
- Profit-sharing

**The Judge:**
- Federal admiralty court
- Decided salvage claims
- Enormous power
- Key institution

### The Process

**The Alert:**
- Lookouts watched reef
- Signal when ship in distress
- Race to reach wreck
- First arrival had advantage

**The Salvage:**
- Rescue passengers first
- Remove cargo
- Sometimes refloat ship
- Bring goods to Key West

**The Award:**
- Cargo stored in warehouses
- Court assessed value
- Awarded percentage to wreckers
- Remainder to ship owner

## The Decline

### The Lighthouses

**The Solution:**
- U.S. built lighthouses on reef
- Navigation improved
- Fewer wrecks
- Industry declined

**Key Lights:**
- Carysfort Reef Light (1852)
- Sand Key Light (1853)
- Sombrero Key Light (1858)
- Reef illuminated

### Other Factors

**Steam Power:**
- Steamships replaced sail
- More maneuverable
- Better navigation
- Fewer wrecks

**The Charts:**
- Better maps
- Navigation improved
- Reef better understood
- Danger reduced

### The End

**By 1900:**
- Wrecking largely over
- Key West sought new industries
- Cigar manufacturing
- Sponge industry
- Tourism eventually
