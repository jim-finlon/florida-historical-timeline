# The Ancient Spanish Monastery: Medieval Europe in North Miami Beach
## 1141/1952-Present

---

## Overview

One of the most improbable structures in Florida is the Ancient Spanish Monastery in North Miami Beachâ€”a medieval Cistercian cloister built in Sacramenia, Spain, in 1141 CE, purchased by William Randolph Hearst in 1925, dismantled stone by stone, shipped to America, and eventually reassembled in Florida. The monastery spent nearly 30 years in 11,000 numbered crates in a Brooklyn warehouse before entrepreneurs bought it, solved the puzzle of reassembly, and opened it in 1953 as a tourist attraction and working Episcopal church. Today, the 12th-century building is the oldest European-built structure in the Western Hemisphere and one of Florida's strangest historical sites.

## William Randolph Hearst

### The Purchase

**1925:**
- Hearst bought the monastery
- Also purchased property in Spain
- Dismantling began
- Planned for San Simeon

**The Cost:**
- $500,000 purchase
- Plus dismantling costs
- Shipping expenses
- Enormous investment

### The Dismantling

**The Process:**
- Each stone numbered
- Carefully taken apart
- Packed in straw in wooden crates
- 11,000 crates total

**The Shipping:**
- Sent to New York
- 1926 arrival
- Stored in Brooklyn
- Waiting for reassembly

## The Quarantine Disaster

### Hoof-and-Mouth Disease

**The Problem:**
- Spain had disease outbreak
- Straw packing material suspect
- U.S. Department of Agriculture
- Quarantine imposed

**The Consequences:**
- Crates opened
- Straw burned
- Stones removed
- Numbering system destroyed

### The Chaos

**The Result:**
- Stones jumbled
- Original numbering lost
- Pieces mixed up
- Giant jigsaw puzzle created
