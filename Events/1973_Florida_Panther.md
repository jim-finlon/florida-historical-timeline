# The Florida Panther: From Near-Extinction to Survival
## 1958-Present

---

## Overview

By the 1970s, the Florida panther—a subspecies of cougar once ranging throughout the Southeast—had been reduced to perhaps 20 individuals hiding in the swamps of South Florida, the last surviving population of cougars east of the Mississippi River. Hunted for bounties, their habitat destroyed by development, panthers seemed destined to join the Caribbean monk seal and Carolina parakeet in extinction. But a remarkable conservation effort, including controversial genetic rescue using Texas cougars, pulled the Florida panther back from the brink. Today, 120-230 panthers roam the Big Cypress and Everglades, and the panther has become Florida's official state animal—a symbol of both the state's environmental destruction and its potential for redemption.

**The Problem:**
- Inbreeding severe

- Reproductive issues

**1995:**
- Eight female Texas cougars introduced
- Same species, different subspecies
- Genetic diversity added
- Experiment begun

**The Results:**
- Hybrid kittens born
- Genetic problems reduced
- Population grew
- Success

### The Controversy
