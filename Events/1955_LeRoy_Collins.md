# LeRoy Collins: Florida's Conscience Governor
## 1955-1961

---

## Overview

Thomas LeRoy Collins served as Florida's governor during the turbulent years of the civil rights movement, and unlike most Southern governors of his era, he refused to embrace massive resistance to desegregation. While never a civil rights crusader, Collins became known as the "South's most moderate governor," calling segregation "morally wrong" in a 1960 statewide addressâ€”words almost unthinkable from a Southern leader of that time. He later walked beside Martin Luther King Jr. at Selma as President Johnson's special representative. Collins paid a political price for his moderation, losing a 1968 Senate race, but earned a place in history as a voice of reason in an age of extremism.

## Governor of Florida

### The Election

**1954:**
- Ran for governor
- Won Democratic primary (then tantamount to election)
- Became governor January 1955
- Re-elected 1956 (first Florida governor to serve two full terms)

### The Context

**The Times:**
- Brown v. Board of Education (1954)
- Massive resistance across South
- White Citizens' Councils
- Pressure for segregation

**Florida's Response:**
- More moderate than Deep South
- But still segregated
- Political pressure intense
- Collins navigated carefully

---

## The Moderate Path

### The Position

**What Collins Did:**
- Refused to close schools (as some states did)
- Kept order
- Rejected extreme segregationist rhetoric
- But didn't integrate either

**The Strategy:**
- Delay but don't defy
- Local control
- Gradual change
- Avoid violence

### The Interposition Crisis

**1957:**
- Florida legislature passed interposition resolution
- Claimed right to nullify federal law
- Collins criticized it as "dishonest"
- Vetoed parts of segregation legislation

**The Courage:**
- Other governors embraced nullification
- Collins called it futile
- Risked political capital
- Stood for law

### Key Moments

**1956 Tallahassee Bus Boycott:**
- Blacks boycotted segregated buses
- Collins avoided violence
- Didn't support boycott but kept peace
- Contrast with other states

**1960 Sit-ins:**
- Student sit-ins across Florida
- Collins called for negotiation
- Didn't use force to stop protests
- Relatively peaceful

---

## The 1960 Address

### "The Most Courageous Speech"

**March 20, 1960:**
- Statewide television address
- Addressed sit-in movement
- Unprecedented words

**The Words:**
- Called segregation "morally wrong"
- Said discrimination could not be legally enforced
- Asked Floridians to examine their hearts
- Shocked the South

**The Quote:**
> "We are going to have to make adjustments in our way of life... it is morally wrong for people to discriminate."

### The Reaction

**The Response:**
- National praise from moderates
- Condemnation from segregationists
- Political risk real
- Collins' standing damaged in some quarters

**The Legacy:**
- One of few Southern governors to say it
- Remembered for moral courage
- Ahead of his time
- Historic moment

## After the Governorship

### National Democratic Party

**1960 Convention:**
- Permanent chairman of Democratic National Convention
- Nominated John F. Kennedy
- National visibility
- Moderate Southern voice

### The LBJ Administration

**Community Relations Service:**
- President Johnson appointed Collins director
- Federal agency to mediate civil rights disputes
- Collins went to Selma (1965)
- Walked with marchers

### The Selma March

**March 1965:**
- Collins sent to Selma as federal mediator
- Negotiated between marchers and Alabama officials
- Walked across Edmund Pettus Bridge
- With Martin Luther King Jr.

**The Photograph:**
- Collins walking with civil rights leaders
- In suit among marchers
- Powerful image
- Would haunt him in Florida

## The 1968 Senate Race

### The Campaign

**Running for Senate:**
- Collins challenged incumbent Senator Spessard Holland
- Later ran in general election
- Democratic nomination contested

**The Attack:**
- Opponent used Selma photo
- Called Collins "Liberal LeRoy"
- Race-baiting campaign
- Old South tactics

### The Defeat

**The Result:**
- Collins lost to Republican Edward Gurney
- Civil rights positions used against him
- Florida not ready
- Price of moderation

## Legacy

### For Civil Rights

**The Significance:**
- Showed not all Southern governors were extremists
- Moderation possible
- Moral voice mattered
- Courage had cost

**The Limits:**
- Collins didn't end segregation
- Moved slowly
- Moderate, not radical
- Product of his time

### For Florida

**The Example:**
- Different from Deep South
- Relative moderation
- Avoided worst violence
- Prepared for change

### For American Politics

**The Lesson:**
- Moral stands matter
- May cost politically
- History judges differently
- Courage remembered
