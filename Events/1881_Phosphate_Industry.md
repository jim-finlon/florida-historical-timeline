# Florida's Phosphate Industry: Mining the Bones of the Earth
## 1881-Present

---

## Overview

In 1881, the discovery of massive phosphate deposits in Florida's central ridge launched an industry that would transform the state's economy and ecology. Phosphate—essential for fertilizer production—lay in ancient seabeds that once covered Florida, concentrated in the bones and teeth of prehistoric marine life. Within a decade of the discovery, Florida became the world's leading phosphate producer, a position it still holds today. The industry brought wealth to central Florida but also left a legacy of environmental damage: vast moonscapes of mined land, contaminated water supplies, and radioactive waste. Phosphate is Florida's most valuable mineral resource and one of its most controversial industries.

## The Mining

### The Methods

**Early Mining:**
- Simple open-pit extraction

- River dredging

**Modern Mining:**
- Draglines (massive excavators)

### The Process

**Extraction:**
- Remove overburden (surface soil)
- Extract matrix (phosphate layer)
- Wash and separate
- Concentrate phosphate

**Processing:**
- Chemical plants
- Sulfuric acid treatment
- Fertilizer production
- Export facilities

**The Bone Valley:**
