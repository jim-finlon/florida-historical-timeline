# The Rosewood Massacre
## January 1-7, 1923

---

## Overview

In the first week of January 1923, a white mob destroyed the predominantly Black town of Rosewood, Florida, in Levy County. The violence began when a white woman in nearby Sumner claimed a Black man had assaulted her. Over the next week, white mobs from surrounding areas descended on Rosewood, burning homes and churches, hunting residents through the swamps, and killing an unknown number of peopleâ€”officially six Black residents and two whites, though survivors believed the true death toll was much higher. The survivors fled and never returned; Rosewood ceased to exist. For decades, the massacre was covered up and forgotten, until survivors came forward in the 1980s, leading to a landmark state investigation and the first state compensation ever paid to victims of racial violence in American history.

## The Setting

### Rosewood in 1923

**The Town:**
- Small African American community
- Levy County, near Cedar Key
- Population: approximately 120-150
- Established after Civil War

**The Economy:**
- Lumber industry
- Turpentine production
- Self-sufficient community
- Some residents owned land and homes

**The Character:**
- Three churches
- School
- General store
- Masonic lodge
- Proud, stable community

### Sumner

**The Neighboring Town:**
- Predominantly white
- Company town for Cummer Lumber Company
- Adjacent to Rosewood
- Racial tensions present

### Racial Climate

**Florida in 1923:**
- Jim Crow laws enforced
- Lynchings occurred regularly
- White supremacy unchallenged
- Violence against Blacks common

**The Context:**
- Post-WWI racial tensions
- Red Summer of 1919 still recent
- Tulsa Race Massacre (1921) had occurred
- Violence could erupt anywhere

## The Story

### The Accusation

**January 1, 1923:**
- Fannie Taylor, white woman in Sumner
- Claimed a Black man had assaulted her
- Some evidence suggests she was covering an affair
- White men began searching for attacker

**The Response:**
- Word spread quickly
- Mobs formed
- Bloodhounds brought in
- Suspicion fell on Jesse Hunter (escaped convict)

### The Violence Begins

**January 1-3:**
- Mobs roamed looking for suspect
- Sam Carter, Black man, captured
- Tortured for information
- Lynched when he couldn't provide it

**Aaron Carrier:**
- Another Black man captured
- Beaten severely
- Survived but badly injured
- Mob growing

### The Attack on Rosewood

**January 4:**
- Mobs converged on Rosewood
- Houses burned
- Residents fled
- Sarah Carrier's home became refuge

**The Carrier House:**
- Sylvester Carrier armed and defended home
- Women and children sheltered inside
- Shootout with mob
- Sylvester and Sarah Carrier killed
- Two white attackers also killed

**January 5-7:**
- Systematic destruction of Rosewood
- Every building burned
- Residents hunted in swamps
- More killings

### The Escape

**The Rescue:**
- John Wright, white store owner, helped some escape
- Train conductors allowed Blacks to board
- Many fled through swamps
- Children hidden and evacuated

**The Survivors:**
- Scattered to Gainesville, Jacksonville, other cities
- Lost everything
- Traumatized
- Never returned

---

## The Aftermath

### The Cover-Up

**Official Response:**
- Governor Cary Hardee sent National Guard (too late)
- Grand jury convened
- "Insufficient evidence" to indict anyone
- No arrests, no prosecutions

**The Silence:**
- Survivors afraid to speak
- White community silent
- Story suppressed
- Rosewood erased from maps

### What Was Lost

**The Town:**
- Every building destroyed
- Church, school, homes gone
- No rebuilding attempted
- Land eventually sold

**The People:**
- Dead: officially 6 Blacks, 2 whites
- Actual toll likely much higher (estimates up to 150)
- Bodies in mass graves or swamps
- Full truth never known

**The Community:**
- Scattered permanently
- Families separated
- Prosperity destroyed
- Generational trauma

---

## The Rediscovery

### Breaking the Silence

**1982:**
- Gary Moore, journalist, began investigating
- Found survivors willing to talk
- Documented the story
- Published articles

**The Survivors:**
- Elderly by 1980s
- Many had kept silence for 60 years
- Finally spoke about what happened
- Preserved the history

### State Investigation

**1993:**
- Florida legislature commissioned investigation
- Academic team researched
- Survivors testified
- Report documented massacre

**The Findings:**
- Confirmed massacre occurred
- Documented destruction
- Named victims
- Recommended compensation

### The Compensation

**1994:**
- Florida legislature passed Rosewood Claims Bill
- First time U.S. state compensated victims of racial violence
- $150,000 to each living survivor (9 at the time)
- Scholarships for descendants
- $500,000 for families who lost property

**The Precedent:**
- Historic acknowledgment
- Model for other cases
- Partial justice after 70 years
- National attention

## Key People

### The Victims

**Sam Carter:**
- First victim
- Lynched January 1 or 2
- Had nothing to do with alleged assault
- Killed for being Black

**Sylvester Carrier:**
- Defended his family
- Killed in shootout at mother's house
- Took two attackers with him
- Died protecting others

**Sarah Carrier:**
- Sylvester's mother
- Killed in the attack on her home
- Home was refuge for women and children

**Many Others:**
- Names lost to history
- Bodies never recovered
- Full count unknown

### The Survivors

**Minnie Lee Langley:**
- Child survivor
- Testified decades later
- Received compensation
- Preserved memory

**Arnett Goins:**
- Survivor who testified
- Helped document the history
- Received compensation

**Lee Ruth Davis:**
- Child survivor
- Last known survivor (died 2020s)
- Kept memory alive

### The Helpers

**John Wright:**
- White store owner in Rosewood
- Hid Black residents
- Helped with evacuation
- Risked his life

**Train Conductors:**
- Allowed Black residents to board
- Some conductors helped evacuation
- Small acts of courage

### The Perpetrators

**The Mob:**
- Largely unnamed
- From Sumner and surrounding areas
- Never prosecuted
- Protected by silence

## Significance

### For Florida History

**The Pattern:**
- Not isolated incident
- Part of widespread racial violence
- Florida had many such events
- Rosewood unusually well-documented

**The Silence:**
- Decades of suppression
- Written out of history
- Required excavation
- Shows how history is erased

### For American History

**The Precedent:**
- First state reparations for racial violence
- Model for other cases
- Acknowledgment of wrong
- Partial justice possible

**The Lesson:**
- Racial violence had devastating consequences
- Communities destroyed
- Trauma transmitted across generations
- Truth eventually emerged

### For Civil Rights

**The Memory:**
- Rosewood became symbol
- Of violence and cover-up
- Of survival and testimony
- Of delayed justice

---

## Legacy

### Commemoration

**The Site:**
- Historical marker placed
- Annual commemorations
- Descendants gather
- Memory preserved

**The Film:**
- *Rosewood* (1997), directed by John Singleton
- Brought story to national audience
- Some historical liberties
- Raised awareness

### The Scholarship

**Academic Attention:**
- Books and articles
- Documentary films
- University research
- Oral histories preserved

### What It Means

Rosewood teaches:

1. **Violence destroys communities:** An entire town erased
2. **Silence enables impunity:** Decades of cover-up
3. **Survivors preserve truth:** Speaking out despite fear
4. **Justice can be partial:** Compensation came, but late
5. **Memory matters:** Forgetting enables repetition
