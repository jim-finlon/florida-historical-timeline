# National Key Deer Refuge: Protecting the Tiniest Deer
## 1957-Present

---

## Overview

The National Key Deer Refuge on Big Pine Key and surrounding islands in the Florida Keys protects the Key deerâ€”a miniature subspecies of white-tailed deer found nowhere else on Earth. Standing only 24-32 inches at the shoulder, these diminutive deer nearly vanished in the 1950s when hunting and habitat loss reduced the population to fewer than 50 animals. Established in 1957, the 9,200-acre refuge has nursed the population back to nearly 1,000 deer while protecting the unique pine rockland and hardwood hammock ecosystems of the Lower Keys. The Key deer have become beloved ambassadors for the Florida Keys, a reminder that even in paradise, wildlife needs protection.

## The Key Deer

### The Species

**The Subspecies:**
- Odocoileus virginianus clavium
- Florida white-tailed deer subspecies
- Endemic to Lower Florida Keys
- Smallest North American deer

**The Size:**
- 24-32 inches shoulder height
- 55-75 pounds (males)
- 45-65 pounds (females)
- Truly tiny deer

### The Appearance

**The Features:**
- Proportionally similar to mainland deer
- Miniaturized frame
- Smaller antlers on males
- Adapted to island life

### The Origin

**The Evolution:**
- Isolated for thousands of years
- Island dwarfism
- Adapted to limited resources
- Unique subspecies
